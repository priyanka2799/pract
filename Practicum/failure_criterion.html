FAILURE CRITERION




About the Experiment
 Fracture or failure involves the forced separation of a material into two or more parts.The purpose of failure criteria is to predict or estimate the failure/yield of machine parts and structural members. Metallurgists and engineers work to find the reasons and mechanisms that caused the failure in the material. In many cases corrosion and mechanical failure are found to be the primary reasons for failure.For ductile materials, the most commonly used failure thoeries are maximum shear stress criterion and the Von Mises criterion. The maximum normal stress criterion and Mohr's thoery are commonly used for brittle materials. This experiment will help you to determine whether the given stress conditions and safety factor will give a ductile fracture, brittle fracture or no failure.




Theory Behind the Experiment

Ductile failure
A ductile failure is a type of failure seen in malleable materials characterized by extensive plastic deformation or necking. This usually occurs prior to the actual failure of the material. In ductile failure, there is absorption of massive amounts of energy and slow propagation before the fracture occurs. So we get indications of failure before it happens, hence it is easier to control. More than 5% elongation is seen in ductile materials before fracture. Examples of ductile material are copper, aluminium, gold, mild steel, etc. The maximum shear stress criterion and the Von Mises criterion are used to predict ductile failure.


The Maximum Shear Stress theory (also known as Tresca criterion) states that failure occurs when the maximum shear stress from a combination of principal stresses equals or exceeds the value obtained for the shear stress at yielding in the uniaxial tensile test. At yielding, in an uni-axial test, the principal stresses are σ1 = Sy; σ2 = 0 and σ3 = 0. Therefore the shear strength at yielding
Ssy =[σ1 - (σ2 or σ3 =0)]/2. Therefore Ssy = Sy/2 To use this theory for either two or three-dimensional static stress in homogeneous, isotopic, ductile materials, first compute the three principal stresses ( σ1, σ2, σ3) and the maximum shear stress τ13 as
τmax = (σ1-σ2)/2
Then compare the maximum shear stress to the failure criterion.
τmax ≤ Ssy
The safety factor for the maximum shear-stress theory is given by
N = Ssy/τmax

The distortion energy theory (also known as Von Mises criterion) states that failure by yielding under a combination of stresses occurs when the energy of distortion equals or exceeds the energy of distortion in the tensile test when the yield strength is reached.
According to theory failure criteria is
Sy = [σ12 + σ22 + σ32 - σ1σ2 - σ2σ3 - σ2σ3]1/2
For two dimensional stress state (σ2 = 0), the equations reduces to
Sy = [σ12 + σ32 - σ1σ3]1/2
The von-Mises effective stress (σe) also sometimes referred to as equivalent stress is defined as the uniaxial tensile stress that would create the same distortion energy as is created by the actual combination of applied stresses.
σe = [σ12 + σ22 + σ32 - σ1σ2 - σ2σ3 - σ2σ3]1/2
σe = [σ12 + σ32 - σ1σ3]1/2
σe = [σxx2 + σyy2 - σxxσyy + 3τxy2]1/2
The safety factor is
N = Sy/σe
Brittle failure
Brittle failure refers to the breakage of a material due to a sudden fracture. Brittle fracture occurs suddenly without any prior indications and hence it is highly necessary to avoid brittle fracture. The fracturing or breaking can occur with only a small amount of load, impact force or shock without much deformation or straining. Brittle materials absorb less energy before breaking or fracturing, despite the materials having a high strength. In general, less than 5% elongation is seen in brittle materials. Brittle materials include cast iron, concrete, ceramic, glass, etc. The maximum normal stress criterion and Mohr's theory are used to predict brittle failure.
The maximum normal stress theorystates that when the maximum normal stress in any direction of a brittle material reaches the strength of the material - the material fails. Thus, finding the principal stresses at critical locations is important. Failure occurs when
σ1 > Sut
The factor of safety is given by
N = Sy/σmax

The Mohr's theory (also known as the Coulomb-Mohr criterion) suggests that failure occurs when Mohr's Circle at a point in the body exceeds the envelope created by the two Mohr's circles for uniaxial tensile strength and uniaxial compression strength.
The factor of safety N can be solved for using
σ1/Sut + σ2/Suc = 1/N
According to this theory, if both σ1 and σ2 are positive, i.e, in tension, both should be less than the ultimate strength in tension to avoid failure. Similarly, if both σ1 and σ2 are negative, i.e, in compression, both should be less than the ultimate strength in compression to avoid failure.
In the case where σ1 is positive and σ2 is negative, we use the value of σ1/Sut - σ2/Suc to determine whether failure occurs. If this quantity is greater than 1, then failure occurs. In the case where σ1 is negative and σ2 is positive, we use the value of σ2/Sut - σ1/Suc to determine whether failure occurs. If this quantity is greater than 1, then failure occurs.




Procedure

For calculating brittile fracture stresses, enter the values of principal stresses (normal stresses) and the values of ultimate strengths in tension and compression. Then press 'Calculate' to obtain the brittle fracture criterion.

For ductile materials, the Von Mises stress can be found by entering the values of principal stresses (normal stresses) and shear stress and get the results using the 'Calculate' button. The values can be cleared using the 'Reset' button.
